# The 2nd Summit - Production Plan

The operating manual for actually making and shipping videos. `BRAND.md`
answers "what is this channel about". `DESIGN.md` answers "what does the site
look like". This document answers the question that decides whether the channel
exists at all: "how do I produce it, consistently, for long enough to matter".

When this document and your motivation on a given day disagree, this document
wins. The whole point of it is to make shipping a decision you already made,
not one you make every week.

## The Core Risk

Most channels die from attrition, not quality. They quit before roughly 20
videos, before the platform has enough data to know who to show the channel to.
The failure is almost never "the videos were bad". It is "the videos stopped".

So the primary metric for the first year is not views, watch time, or
subscribers. It is:

> Did the video ship this week?

Everything below exists to protect that one number.

## Operating Principle: Run the Channel Like an Engineering Project

This is also the channel's main differentiator (see "Engineer Your Life" in
`BRAND.md`), so the production system is meant to be eaten as your own dog food.
Treat the channel as a system with a backlog, a cadence, a build pipeline, and a
definition of done. Reduce per-video decisions to near zero. Decisions are where
motivation leaks out.

## Cadence

Pick one cadence and hold it for 12 months before judging results.

- Default commitment: one video per week, same publish day and time.
- Realistic fallback if weekly proves unsustainable after a month: one video
  every two weeks. Do not change cadence more than once. A stable cadence the
  algorithm and audience can predict beats a faster one you abandon.
- Publish on the same weekday every week. Consistency of schedule matters more
  than the specific day.

A channel that ships 50 mediocre-to-good videos in a year beats one that ships 8
polished ones and stops. Volume is how you find your voice, your audience, and
the algorithm's favor. Aim for finished, not perfect.

## The Pipeline (Idea to Published)

Treat every video as moving through fixed stages. A video is only ever in one
stage. The backlog lives in one place (a single board or list, not your head).

1. Idea: a one-line title and the promise. Must pass the Critical Content Rule
   in `BRAND.md` ("would someone learn something useful from this?").
2. Outline: the four-beat Discovery framework or the format's framework. One
   page maximum.
3. Film: capture all footage. No editing yet.
4. Edit: assemble, cut, captions.
5. Package: title, thumbnail, description, end screen, tags.
6. Schedule: upload, set publish date, done.

Keep a buffer. The target is to always have at least 2 to 3 finished videos
scheduled ahead of the publish date. The buffer is what carries you through a
sick week, a travel week, or a week you simply do not feel like it. Building the
buffer is the first job, ahead of growth.

## Batching

Solo creators burn out doing all six pipeline stages every single week. Batch by
stage instead.

- Film in batches: shoot 3 to 4 videos in one session while lighting, location,
  and energy are already set up. Many of your topics (gym, Strand walks,
  pickleball) are things you already do, so capture footage opportunistically
  and bank it.
- Edit in a dedicated block, not interleaved with filming.
- Package in a block: write all titles and make thumbnails together so they stay
  visually consistent and you stay in one headspace.

Batching converts a weekly creative cold-start into a few focused work sessions
per month.

## Time Budget

Write down a realistic per-video time cost and protect it on the calendar like a
meeting. A first pass estimate for a solo creator:

- Outline: 30 to 60 minutes
- Film: 1 to 2 hours (less when batched)
- Edit: 3 to 6 hours (this is the one that balloons; cap it deliberately)
- Package: 1 to 2 hours

Editing is where time and motivation drain fastest. Set a hard cap. If a cut is
not done at the cap, it ships as is or gets simplified, it does not get a third
weekend. Perfection in the edit is the most expensive and least rewarded habit
you can build.

## Minimum Viable Video

Define the smallest thing you are still willing to publish, so a bad week still
produces a ship instead of a skip.

- A direct-to-camera "Lessons Learned" piece (see `BRAND.md` formats): one good
  idea, clean audio, decent light, 4 to 8 minutes, minimal editing.
- Non-negotiables even at minimum: clear audio, a title that makes a promise,
  and one genuinely useful takeaway.
- When in doubt, ship the minimum viable video. A shipped MVV beats a perfect
  video that misses the week.

## On-Camera Skill

This is the highest-leverage variable in the entire channel and the one the
brand and design docs do not cover. The "honest experimenter" positioning means
the channel cannot lean on guru authority, so it leans on you being watchable,
warm, and clear. That is a skill, and skills are built with reps.

- Treat your first 10 to 20 videos as deliberate practice, not your best work.
  Expect to be stiff early. Everyone is.
- Watch your own footage back honestly, or hand it to someone who will be blunt.
  Note one thing to improve per video (pace, filler words, energy, eye line).
- Energy on camera reads about 20 percent lower than it feels. Push past what
  feels natural.
- Do not let "I do not like how I come across yet" become the reason to stop.
  That feeling is the cost of the first 20 videos, not a verdict on the channel.

## Launch Scope: Start Narrow

`BRAND.md` defines five pillars. That is the full map, not the starting point.
Opening across all five sends the algorithm five different audience signals and
slows down its ability to find your people.

- Launch focused on a single lane for the first 10 to 15 videos. Recommended
  spine: the engineering and systems lens applied to one concrete area (fitness
  and daily movement, or the purpose and isolation material), because that lane
  is your clearest differentiator and least saturated.
- Once the channel has a recognizable identity and the algorithm knows who to
  serve, widen into the other pillars deliberately, one at a time.
- Breadth is earned after the channel has a center of gravity, not assumed at
  the start.

## Sequencing With the Website

The website is finished enough. The content engine is not yet proven. Until it
is, the website is a distraction dressed as progress.

- Publish at least 5 videos before making any further changes to `DESIGN.md` or
  the site. Those 5 videos generate the only data that matters right now:
  whether you will actually do this, and whether you are watchable.
- The planned-report cards on the site (see `DESIGN.md`) are a promise. Every
  pillar shown as "PLANNED" is a debt to the audience. Do not announce more than
  you intend to ship.

## Definition of Success (First 12 Months)

Judge the channel against the goal that is actually within your control, not
against viral breakout (which is mostly luck, charisma, and volume, and is the
wrong bar for year one).

A successful first year looks like:

- A consistent cadence held for 12 months.
- A real body of useful work (40 to 50 videos at weekly, 20 to 25 biweekly).
- Visible improvement in on-camera presence from video 1 to video 40.
- A small but genuinely engaged audience, and the email list growing (the only
  audience asset the site owns; see `DESIGN.md` Pointer Site Rules).

Subscriber count and revenue are lagging outputs of the above, not the target.
If the four things above happen, you have built the second summit you described
in `BRAND.md`, regardless of what the view counter says.

## Review Cadence

Run a monthly retro on the channel like you would on a project:

- Did every scheduled video ship? If not, why, and what in this plan failed?
- Which video performed best and worst, and what is the honest guess as to why?
- Is the buffer still at 2 to 3 videos? If it is shrinking, that is the early
  warning sign of burnout. Address it before the streak breaks, not after.

Adjust the plan deliberately at the monthly retro. Do not adjust it in the
middle of a bad week.
